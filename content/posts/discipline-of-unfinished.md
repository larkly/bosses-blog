There is a particular pleasure in a half-finished thing. The note scrawled in the margin,
the paragraph ending mid-thought, the sketch abandoned before its shadows were filled in.
For most of my life I treated these as failures — evidence of a lack of finishing muscle.
I now think they were closer to the point than anything I later polished.

## The myth of the finished draft

Every writing manual I've read insists on the sanctity of the finished draft. *Ship it,*
they say. *Done is better than perfect.* And there is a certain truth there — I have
watched friends spend years tuning a single essay while the world moved past them. But
there's a shadow side: the fetish of completion that makes us sand off every interesting
edge until the whole thing gleams and says nothing.

The finished draft is a social object. It's what you bring to a dinner party, what you
submit to an editor, what you can point to and say *I made that.* The unfinished draft
is a private one. It's what you talk to yourself through. It's where you're still
actually thinking.

## Three kinds of unfinished

Not all incompleteness is the same. I've come to distinguish three kinds:

1. **The abandoned** — things I gave up on because the thinking went sour. These
   sometimes return years later, shaped by whatever the sourness taught me.
2. **The paused** — things I left because I wasn't ready yet. The argument was there
   but I wasn't the one who could make it. I needed to become someone else first.
3. **The refused** — things I could finish, but choose not to. The ending would wreck
   the thing. The reader's imagination is better than any closing I could write.

It's the third kind I've learned to protect. They're the strangest and the most alive.

## How to know when to stop

There's no reliable external signal. Deadlines help but also lie. Editors help but
have their own schedules. What I've found useful:

- Does a fresh reading make me want to cut, or make me want to keep going? Cut instinct
  means the thing is full. Keep-going instinct means it isn't.
- Can I name what would be lost by finishing? If I can name it clearly, that's usually
  the signal to stop.
- Does the ending I'm contemplating feel earned or manufactured? Manufactured endings
  turn essays into packages, and packages are boring.

## Against the myth of polish

A perfectly polished piece of writing is a closed system. There is nothing left for
the reader to do. They are a consumer, not a collaborator. The pieces I return to — the
ones that have shaped how I see things — all had some kind of aperture. A loose thread.
An implication the writer gestured at but didn't close.

That's the discipline I'm interested in now. Not *finish what you start* but *know what
not to finish.* Leave the thread. Let the reader do their share of the work.

The half-finished thing respects its reader. The polished thing mistrusts them.
