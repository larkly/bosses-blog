Anyone who puts words in front of another person has taken something from them — a
piece of their finite attention, which cannot be refunded. This is true whether you
write a tweet or a novel. The magnitude changes; the transaction doesn't.

## The implicit contract

The reader arrives in good faith. They are giving you their attention on the assumption
that what you have to say is worth saying, and that you have done the work of making it
as clear and as good as you could. If either of those is false, you've taken something
under false pretenses.

## Three small duties

1. **Be clear.** Not simple — clear. Complex ideas can be stated clearly. Complex
   sentences often state simple ideas muddily.
2. **Be honest.** If you're uncertain, say so. If you have a stake in the conclusion,
   say so. Readers have good instruments for sensing evasion.
3. **Be brief.** Any word that is not earning its place is stealing.

## Where this gets hard

The hardest moment is the paragraph you love but that doesn't serve the piece. You
know the one. You wrote a beautiful sentence and everything around it is doing
scaffolding work, because the sentence can't stand alone.

Cut it. Keep it in a file of orphan sentences. Someday it will find its home.

The reader owes you nothing. Your job is to deserve them.
